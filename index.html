Purpose

What students should understand on Day One

The Navigator is not a grading machine, a personality test, a therapist, or a moral judge. It is a private space for slowing down, mapping situations, identifying assumptions, comparing possible moves, and preparing better ethical deliberation.

Core message for students: The tool matters less than the practice. Students can use The Navigator, a printed worksheet, or their own notebook. The course goal is to internalize a disciplined sequence of attention, context, alternatives, evaluation, and responsible judgment.

Week 1 handout

The Navigator and Critical Thinking: From Reflection to Reasoned Judgment

The Navigator is a private reflection tool, but it is not separate from critical thinking. It is one way of practicing critical thinking in the concrete situations of ordinary life. Traditional critical thinking courses often teach students to identify assumptions, clarify claims, examine evidence, consider alternatives, detect fallacies, evaluate reasons, and reach better-supported conclusions. Ethics courses ask students to apply those skills to questions of value, responsibility, harm, care, justice, duty, character, and consequence. The Navigator brings these two tasks together.

Critical thinking begins when we stop treating our first reaction as the whole truth. A first reaction may be important. It may reveal fear, anger, care, loyalty, unfairness, confusion, or concern. But a first reaction still needs examination. The Navigator helps students pause and ask: What am I noticing? What am I assuming? What might I be missing? What other fields of life are involved? Who else is affected? What values are at stake?

The Field Scan is the first critical thinking move. A student maps one area of life—school, work, family, health, friendship, community, future planning, or identity—and asks what is happening there. This is similar to defining the problem in critical thinking.

The combined context is the second critical thinking move. Real ethical problems usually involve more than one field. A work decision may affect school. A family obligation may affect health. A friendship conflict may affect honesty, loyalty, stress, and self-respect. The Navigator helps students assemble these fields into a wider context.

Move Cards are the third critical thinking move. Instead of asking students to leap from confusion to a final answer, The Navigator asks them to generate possible next moves. This resembles the critical thinking practice of considering alternatives.

Comparing moves across fields is the fourth critical thinking move. In logic and argument analysis, students learn that a conclusion must be tested against reasons, objections, and consequences. In ethics, a possible action must be tested across values and relationships.

The Current Move Plan is the fifth critical thinking move. Critical thinking is not merely criticism. It should help us reach a more responsible judgment. A Current Move Plan asks the student to choose one small, deliberate next step, explain why it makes sense, identify the values it protects, notice the risks it carries, and decide when to review it.

AI-optional principle: Students who choose not to use AI can still use the same process on paper. The value lies in the disciplined sequence of attention, context, alternatives, evaluation, and judgment.

That is critical thinking in ethical life. The Navigator simply gives students a structured way to practice it.

Instructor view

Day One facilitation notes

Recommended opening language: “The purpose of this course is not to force disclosure. The purpose is to practice clearer ethical attention, better critical thinking, and more responsible public reasoning. Private reflection stays private unless you choose to use it.”

Discussion prompts

  1. Why might a first reaction be important but incomplete?
  2. What is the difference between a private experience and a public ethical question?
  3. How do assumptions shape what we notice first?
  4. Why should we generate more than one possible move before deciding?
  5. How can students practice this process without AI or digital tools?

Low-stakes closing activity

Ask students to write one public ethical question that emerged from the practice. They should not share private details. Examples: “How should we balance honesty and care?” “When does responsibility require asking for help?” “How should pressure affect moral judgment?”
